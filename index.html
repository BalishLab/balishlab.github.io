About Our Lab

Mycoplasmas are unusual bacteria that lack cell walls and possess the smallest genomes among independently culturable organisms. Many species are important human pathogens causing chronic infections with rising antibiotic resistance worldwide.

Mycoplasma pneumoniae biofilm structures

Our laboratory investigates the fundamental biology of mycoplasmas to understand how these minimal organisms cause disease and persist in their hosts. This knowledge is critical for developing new therapeutic strategies to combat mycoplasma infections affecting millions of people annually.

Research Areas

Graduate and undergraduate students in our lab use diverse cell biological, molecular biological, and biochemical techniques to investigate mycoplasma pathogenesis. Our research focuses on several overlapping areas:

1. Structural Features of Mycoplasma Cells

We study attachment organelles—prominent polar protrusions used by pathogenic species like Mycoplasma pneumoniae and M. genitalium for host cell adherence and surface motility. These structures contain novel cytoskeletal proteins unique to mycoplasmas and show convergent evolution across different species groups.

2. Regulation of Virulence Factor Production

We investigate how M. pneumoniae biofilms regulate virulence factors including hydrogen peroxide, hydrogen sulfide, and CARDS toxin. Understanding these regulatory mechanisms is essential for combating chronic mycoplasma disease.

3. Host-Pathogen Interactions

Different mycoplasma species interact with hosts in distinct ways—from exclusively extracellular (M. pneumoniae) to invasive (M. penetrans). We characterize these interactions to understand infection progression and identify therapeutic targets.

4. Cell Division Mechanisms

Without cell walls, mycoplasmas cannot use typical bacterial division mechanisms. We study how wall-less bacteria accomplish cell division, particularly in species lacking attachment organelles—a fundamental question in bacterial cell biology.

Why This Matters

Mycoplasma infections are highly prevalent, long-lasting, lack vaccines, and face rising antibiotic resistance. Our research aims to provide critical insights that will lead to new therapeutic agents improving the lives of millions suffering from chronic mycoplasma infections.
